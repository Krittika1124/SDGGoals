Causes of Climate Change

  • Greenhouse Effect
  • Fossil Fuels
  • Deforestation

Greenhouse Effect

Natural Greenhouse Effect:

The greenhouse effect occurs when certain gases in the Earth's atmosphere trap heat radiated from the Earth's surface.
These gases, including water vapor (H2O), carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases, act like the glass panels of a greenhouse, allowing sunlight to enter and warming the interior.
Without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average surface temperature would be much colder, making it inhospitable to life as we know it. The natural greenhouse effect maintains Earth's temperature within a range suitable for supporting diverse ecosystems and human civilization.



Human-Enhanced Greenhouse Effect:

Human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels (such as coal, oil, and natural gas), deforestation, industrial processes, and agriculture, have significantly increased the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere since the Industrial Revolution.
The combustion of fossil fuels releases large amounts of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, while deforestation reduces the number of trees that can absorb CO2 through photosynthesis. Methane emissions from livestock, landfills, and agricultural practices also contribute to the enhanced greenhouse effect.

Fossil Fuels

Carbon Dioxide Emissions:

When fossil fuels are burned for energy production, transportation, industrial processes, and other purposes, they release carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere. CO2 is the most significant greenhouse gas emitted through human activities and contributes to the enhanced greenhouse effect, trapping heat and leading to global warming.

Methane and Other Emissions:
In addition to carbon dioxide, the combustion of fossil fuels also releases other greenhouse gases, such as methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O), as well as air pollutants like sulfur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx). Methane, in particular, is a potent greenhouse gas with a much higher warming potential than CO2 over a shorter time frame, though it is less abundant in the atmosphere.

Deforestation and Land Use Change:
Fossil fuels are not only burned directly for energy but also used in various industrial processes, including cement production and land clearing for agriculture and urbanization. These activities often result in deforestation, which reduces the number of trees available to absorb CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis, contributing to increased greenhouse gas concentrations.

Ocean Acidification:
CO2 emissions from the burning of fossil fuels also contribute to ocean acidification, as the oceans absorb a significant portion of atmospheric CO2. Increased CO2 levels in seawater lead to a decrease in pH, which can have harmful effects on marine life, including coral reefs, shellfish, and plankton, with cascading impacts on marine ecosystems and fisheries.

Deforestation

Carbon Emissions:
Trees store large amounts of carbon in their biomass and soil. When forests are cleared or degraded through deforestation, the stored carbon is released into the atmosphere as CO2, primarily through burning or decomposition of vegetation. This process contributes to increased concentrations of greenhouse gases, leading to the enhanced greenhouse effect and global warming.

Loss of Carbon Sinks:
Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis and storing it in tree biomass and soil organic matter. Deforestation reduces the size of these carbon sinks, diminishing their capacity to remove CO2 from the atmosphere and mitigate climate change. This loss of carbon storage exacerbates the impacts of greenhouse gas emissions and contributes to the acceleration of global warming.

Altered Land Surface:
Deforestation alters the land surface and disrupts natural ecosystems, leading to changes in surface albedo, hydrological cycles, and regional climate patterns. For example, the removal of forests can increase surface reflectivity (albedo), leading to more absorption of solar radiation and higher temperatures in deforested areas. Changes in land cover also affect local and regional precipitation patterns, leading to alterations in weather and climate.

Impacts on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services:
Deforestation not only contributes to climate change but also has significant impacts on biodiversity and ecosystem services. Forests are home to a vast array of plant and animal species, many of which are threatened by habitat loss and fragmentation. Deforestation also reduces the provision of ecosystem services such as carbon sequestration, water purification, soil stabilization, and biodiversity conservation, with far-reaching consequences for human well-being and ecological balance.
